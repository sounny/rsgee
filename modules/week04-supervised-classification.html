Week 4: Supervised Classification

Learning objectives: Understand supervised classification workflows, create training samples, apply machine learning classifiers, and distinguish between land use and land cover.

What Is Image Classification?

At its core, image classification is about sorting pixels in an image into different categories or classes. Think of a satellite photo of a city‚Äîthere are roads, buildings, parks, and water bodies. The main goal of image classification is to automatically tell the computer how to distinguish between these different features.

For example, when looking at a Landsat 8 image of the Earth, you see a beautiful tapestry of colors and shapes, but what does it all mean? Image classification helps you sort those pixels into categories like "forest," "water," and "urban area."

This module is what people normally associate a remote sensing class with. We have been building up our skillsets for this moment‚Äîlearning JavaScript, understanding spectral indices, working with image collections, and applying thresholds. Now we put it all together.

Supervised Classification

In supervised classification, you play an active role in guiding the algorithm. You provide a training sample‚Äîa set of points where you know the land cover. The algorithm then uses this sample to classify the rest of the image.

Think of it like teaching a toddler shapes: By showing them examples of circles, squares, and triangles, they eventually learn to identify these shapes independently. When you create a training sample, you make the template for the AI to learn from.

Workflow in Earth Engine

  1. Assemble Features: Collect training data with known land cover classes
  2. Instantiate Classifier: Choose an algorithm (Random Forest, CART, SVM, etc.)
  3. Train the Classifier: Feed training data to the algorithm
  4. Apply Classifier: Classify the entire image
  5. Assess Accuracy: Validate results with testing data
// Example: Basic supervised classification workflow
var trainingData = forest.merge(water).merge(urban);

var classifier = ee.Classifier.smileRandomForest(10)
    .train({
        features: trainingData,
        classProperty: 'landcover',
        inputProperties: ['B2', 'B3', 'B4', 'B5', 'B6', 'B7']
    });

var classified = image.classify(classifier);

Why Choose a Classification Method?

Here's the big question: Which method should you use for classification? A lot of people stick with what they know‚Äîmaybe they learned a particular method in a class or found it easier to implement. While comfort is great, it doesn't necessarily mean that method suits your specific project.

Good research often involves testing multiple methods to see how they stack against each other. Each classification approach has pros and cons:

  • Random Forest: Robust, handles high-dimensional data well, less prone to overfitting
  • CART (Decision Trees): Simple, interpretable, fast, but can overfit
  • SVM (Support Vector Machine): Effective in high-dimensional spaces, good with limited training data
  • Maximum Likelihood: Traditional parametric approach, assumes normal distribution

Land Use vs. Land Cover

LAND USE ‚â† LAND COVER

Understanding land use and cover concepts is essential for image classification:

Land Cover refers to the physical and biophysical properties of the Earth's surface, such as vegetation, water bodies, and impervious surfaces, which can be identified through remote sensing techniques. By analyzing and categorizing these land covers, we gain valuable information about the state and condition of different regions.

Land Use refers to the human activities on the land and how they interact with the land cover. Land use encompasses various activities such as agriculture, urbanization, industrialization, transportation, and recreation. Land use is often related to social, economic, and cultural factors, and it can vary significantly across regions and countries.

Examples

  • Forest (cover): Could be used for logging, conservation, recreation, or hunting (use)
  • Grass (cover): Could be used for livestock grazing in rural areas or parks in urban areas (use)
  • Water (cover): Could be used for hydropower, fishing, transportation, or recreation (use)

Be cautious when assigning human activities to land cover categories, as it involves interpretation and could be subject to error.

Data Access and Preparation

Before you jump into classification, you need to find the right data. Whether using Google Earth Engine, ENVI, ERDAS, ArcGIS, or another platform, you'll spend significant time searching for, downloading, and preparing your data.

With Google Earth Engine, you can access a lot of pre-processed data, making life easier. The platform provides:

  • Atmospherically corrected imagery (Surface Reflectance products)
  • Cloud-masked collections
  • Analysis-ready data (ARD)
  • Consistent data formats across sensors

Quality of Data is Crucial: Whether it's supervised or unsupervised classification, the quality of your data determines your results. Always aim for accurate and representative training samples that cover the full range of spectral variability within each class.

Evolution of Classification Methods

Over time, classification methods have evolved. Initially, we had limited data and simpler algorithms. With better sensors and higher-resolution imagery, we've developed more advanced methods like object-based image analysis and deep learning approaches.

It's an exciting time to be in this field! There are many research opportunities to compare methods and see which ones work for which scenarios. With advancements in AI and machine learning, we can expect even more sophisticated classification methods in the future.

Practical Tips

  1. Training Sample Quality: Collect diverse, representative samples across the full extent of your study area
  2. Class Balance: Try to have similar numbers of training points for each class
  3. Spectral Separability: Choose classes that are spectrally distinct from each other
  4. Validation Data: Keep separate testing data to assess accuracy‚Äînever use training data for validation
  5. Iteration: Classification is iterative; refine your training data based on results

Remember: Every pixel tells a story; your job is to figure out what that story is.
