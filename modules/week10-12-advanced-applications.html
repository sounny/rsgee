Case Study 1: River Morphology

The Power of Planview Geometry

When you look at a river from above—what we call its "planview geometry"—you're essentially peering into a wealth of information about that river system. It's like a fingerprint, unique to each river, revealing intricate details about its life story.

Scientific Foundation

One well-established theory in fluvial geomorphology is that of hydraulic geometry. It suggests a power-law relation, known as a "rating curve," between a river's width and discharge. This insight has profound modern applications:

  • Remote Discharge Monitoring: Measure river width from satellites → estimate discharge
  • Ungauged Basins: Monitor rivers where in-situ measurements are impossible
  • Historical Analysis: Track river changes over decades using imagery archives
  • Flood Prediction: Understand channel capacity and overflow risk

River Meandering and Change

Rivers are not static; they are living organisms that evolve. As water moves from higher elevations to lower ones, rivers adjust their courses through meandering—lateral shifts driven by erosion and accretion of sediment along banks.

Technological Evolution

Early Days: Manual Interpretation (1960s-1990s)

Scientists relied on aerial imagery, manually interpreted to deduce river characteristics—labor-intensive and time-consuming.

Digital Revolution: Automated Processing (2000s-2010s)

RivWidth software (Pavelsky and Smith 2008) first presented automated methods for river width extraction using spectral signatures to identify water boundaries.

Cloud Computing Era: Large-Scale Analysis (2010s-present)

RivWidthCloud (RWC) (Yang et al. 2019) applied and expanded these methods to Earth Engine, enabling:

  • Basin-scale analysis (entire Amazon, Mississippi systems)
  • Multi-decadal time series
  • Automated change detection
  • Real-time monitoring capabilities

Key Analysis Steps

  1. Water Masking: Identify water pixels using spectral indices (NDWI, MNDWI)
  2. Centerline Extraction: Find river centerline using skeleton algorithms
  3. Width Calculation: Measure perpendicular distances across river at regular intervals
  4. Temporal Analysis: Compare widths across time to detect changes
  5. Bank Erosion: Identify areas of lateral migration and erosion

Lab Focus: You'll use Landsat archive to measure river width changes, identify meandering patterns, and quantify bank erosion rates.

Case Study 2: Global Snow Observatory

Why Snow Cover Matters

Snow is a critical component of the Earth's water cycle and climate system:

  • Water Resources: Snowmelt provides ~75% of freshwater in western US
  • Climate Indicator: Snow cover extent tracks global warming
  • Albedo Effect: White snow reflects sunlight, cooling the planet
  • Drought Prediction: Low snow years often precede severe droughts

About MODIS

The Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) aboard Terra and Aqua satellites provides ideal data for snow monitoring:

  • Daily Coverage: Global observations every 1-2 days
  • Long Record: 2000-present (20+ years)
  • Spatial Resolution: 500m (good for regional analysis)
  • Snow Products: Pre-processed snow cover datasets

Analysis Workflow

  1. Load MODIS Snow Cover: Access MOD10A1 (Terra) or MYD10A1 (Aqua) collections
  2. Cloud Masking: Remove cloudy observations to reduce noise
  3. Binary Classification: Convert to snow/no-snow (threshold NDSI > 0.4)
  4. Temporal Compositing: Create 8-day or monthly composites
  5. Snow Frequency Calculation: Percentage of time each pixel is snow-covered
  6. Trend Analysis: Identify areas with declining snow cover over time
// Example: Calculate annual snow frequency
var modisSnow = ee.ImageCollection('MODIS/006/MOD10A1')
    .filterDate('2020-01-01', '2020-12-31')
    .select('NDSI_Snow_Cover');

// Classify as snow (1) or no snow (0)
var binarySnow = modisSnow.map(function(img) {
    return img.gt(50).rename('snow');  // >50% snow cover
});

// Calculate snow frequency (days with snow / total days)
var snowFrequency = binarySnow.sum()
    .divide(binarySnow.count())
    .multiply(100);

Map.addLayer(snowFrequency, {
    min: 0, max: 100,
    palette: ['brown', 'white', 'blue']
}, 'Snow Frequency %');

Lab Focus: Monitor seasonal snow cover patterns, calculate snow-free date trends, and assess climate change impacts on mountain snowpack.

Case Study 3: Urban Heat Islands

The Urban Heat Island Effect

Urban Heat Islands (UHI) occur when cities experience significantly higher temperatures than surrounding rural areas. This phenomenon has serious implications for:

  • Public Health: Heat-related illness and mortality
  • Energy Consumption: Increased cooling demands
  • Air Quality: Accelerated smog formation
  • Environmental Justice: Low-income areas often experience higher temperatures

Causes of Urban Heat Islands

  • Impervious Surfaces: Asphalt and concrete absorb and retain heat
  • Reduced Vegetation: Less evaporative cooling from plants
  • Building Geometry: Tall buildings trap heat in "urban canyons"
  • Waste Heat: Air conditioning, vehicles, industry
  • Reduced Sky View: Less radiation to space at night

Remote Sensing of Land Surface Temperature

Deriving LST from Landsat

Landsat thermal bands (Band 10, 11) measure thermal infrared radiation:

  1. Load Thermal Bands: Access Landsat 8 Collection 2 Level 2
  2. Convert to Brightness Temperature: Apply scaling factors and thermal constants
  3. Calculate Emissivity: Estimate from NDVI (vegetation has high emissivity)
  4. Atmospheric Correction: Account for atmospheric absorption
  5. Calculate LST: Final temperature in Celsius or Kelvin

Defining Urban and Rural References

To quantify UHI intensity, you need reference temperatures:

  • Urban Reference: Mean temperature in city center (high density, low vegetation)
  • Rural Reference: Mean temperature in surrounding countryside (low density, high vegetation)
  • UHI Intensity: Difference between urban and rural temperatures

Analysis Techniques

  • Regression Analysis: Relationship between LST and NDVI, imperviousness
  • Spatial Autocorrelation: Hot spot identification
  • Time Series: Seasonal and diurnal patterns
  • Scenario Modeling: Impact of green infrastructure on cooling

Lab Focus: Derive land surface temperature from Landsat and MODIS, calculate UHI intensity, analyze cooling effects of urban vegetation.

Case Study 4: Wildfire Monitoring and Analysis

The Growing Wildfire Challenge

Wildfires have become more frequent and severe due to climate change, drought, and human activities. Remote sensing provides critical capabilities for:

  • Active Fire Detection: Near real-time monitoring of burning areas
  • Burn Severity Assessment: Quantifying ecological damage
  • Smoke Detection: Air quality and health impacts
  • Recovery Monitoring: Vegetation regrowth after fire
  • Risk Mapping: Identifying high-risk areas

Fire Datasets in Earth Engine

MODIS Active Fire Product

  • Dataset: MODIS/006/MOD14A1 and MYD14A1
  • Temporal Resolution: Daily
  • Detection: Thermal anomalies indicating active fires
  • Use Case: Near real-time fire monitoring

VIIRS Active Fire

  • Dataset: FIRMS (Fire Information for Resource Management System)
  • Resolution: 375m (higher than MODIS)
  • Detection: Both flaming and smoldering fires
  • Use Case: More detailed active fire location

Burned Area Products

  • Dataset: MODIS/006/MCD64A1 (monthly burned area)
  • Use Case: Historical fire perimeter mapping

Burn Severity Analysis

The Normalized Burn Ratio (NBR) quantifies fire severity:

// Calculate NBR
var nbr = image.normalizedDifference(['B5', 'B7']);  // (NIR - SWIR) / (NIR + SWIR)

// Calculate dNBR (difference NBR: pre-fire minus post-fire)
var preFire = preFireImage.normalizedDifference(['B5', 'B7']);
var postFire = postFireImage.normalizedDifference(['B5', 'B7']);
var dNBR = preFire.subtract(postFire);

// Classify burn severity
var burnSeverity = dNBR.where(dNBR.lt(0.1), 1)      // Unburned
    .where(dNBR.gte(0.1).and(dNBR.lt(0.27)), 2)   // Low
    .where(dNBR.gte(0.27).and(dNBR.lt(0.44)), 3)  // Moderate-low
    .where(dNBR.gte(0.44).and(dNBR.lt(0.66)), 4)  // Moderate-high
    .where(dNBR.gte(0.66), 5);                     // High
