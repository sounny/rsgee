Week 9: Research Project Planning

Learning objectives: Develop geographic research questions suited for remote sensing, formulate methodological approaches, state testable hypotheses, and understand the research process from question to publication.

Doing Research with Remote Sensing

Geography is the study of the earth's surface and features, including its physical and cultural landscapes. Remote sensing is a powerful tool that enables geographers to collect data about the earth's surface from a distance without visiting the site physically. This technology has revolutionized how geographers study the world, providing an efficient and effective way to answer research questions.

However, to make the most of remote sensing technology, it is essential to:

  • Develop research questions that are easily answered through remote sensing
  • Formulate a methodological approach for collecting and analyzing data
  • State expected hypotheses based on existing knowledge
  • Consider broader impacts and real-world applications

This week focuses on transforming your Earth Engine skills into meaningful research contributions.

Developing Research Questions for Remote Sensing

The first step in developing research questions for remote sensing is to identify areas where remote sensing can be used effectively.

Where Remote Sensing Excels

  • Land Use and Land Cover Changes: Deforestation, urbanization, agricultural expansion
  • Vegetation Dynamics: Phenology, drought stress, biomass estimation
  • Urbanization Patterns: City growth, sprawl, density changes
  • Water Resources: Lake levels, river width, wetland extent
  • Disaster Monitoring: Flood extent, wildfire severity, landslide detection
  • Climate Indicators: Snow cover, glacial retreat, temperature patterns
  • Coastal Changes: Erosion, sediment transport, mangrove health

Characteristics of Good Research Questions

Once you've identified an area of study, develop research questions that are:

  1. Specific: Focused on a particular phenomenon, location, and time period
  2. Measurable: Can be answered with quantitative or qualitative data
  3. Relevant: Addresses a gap in knowledge or a real-world problem
  4. Feasible: Answerable with available data and methods
  5. Significant: Has implications beyond the immediate study area

    6. Example Research Questions

    ❌ Weak Question:

    "How has land use changed?"

    Problem: Too vague—no location, time period, or specific type of change specified.

    ✓ Strong Question:

    "How has land use and land cover changed in the Amazon Rainforest between 2010 and 2020, and what are the primary drivers of deforestation?"

    Strength: Specific location, defined time period, measurable change, and addresses causation.

    ✓ More Examples of Strong Questions:

    • "What is the relationship between urban heat island intensity and vegetation cover in Phoenix, Arizona during summer months (2015-2024)?"
    • "How has the width of the Mississippi River changed in response to major flood events over the past 30 years?"
    • "Can NDVI time series predict crop yield in Iowa corn fields at a county level?"
    • "What is the spatial extent of glacier retreat in the Himalayas between 1990 and 2023?"

Formulating a Methodological Approach

Once you have developed research questions, the next step is formulating a methodological approach to answer those questions. This approach should include a detailed plan for collecting and analyzing remote sensing data.

Step 1: Identify Data Requirements

Type of Remote Sensing Data

  • Optical Imagery: Landsat, Sentinel-2, MODIS (for vegetation, land cover)
  • Radar: Sentinel-1, ALOS PALSAR (for water, structure, all-weather)
  • Thermal: Landsat thermal bands, MODIS LST (for temperature)
  • Elevation: SRTM, ASTER GDEM (for topography, hydrology)
  • Climate: CHIRPS, TerraClimate (for precipitation, evapotranspiration)

Spatial Resolution Considerations

  • High-resolution (< 10m): Sentinel-2, Planet, WorldView - For detailed local studies
  • Medium-resolution (10-100m): Landsat, Sentinel - For regional studies
  • Coarse-resolution (> 100m): MODIS - For global or continental studies

Temporal Resolution Considerations

  • High-frequency (daily): MODIS, Sentinel - For rapid change detection
  • Medium-frequency (weekly): Landsat composite - For seasonal patterns
  • Low-frequency (annual): Single-date imagery - For long-term trends

Step 2: Data Preprocessing

Outline necessary preprocessing steps:

  • Cloud Masking: Remove cloudy pixels
  • Atmospheric Correction: Convert to surface reflectance
  • Geometric Correction: Ensure spatial accuracy
  • Mosaicking: Combine multiple images
  • Temporal Compositing: Create cloud-free composites

Step 3: Analysis Methods

Describe analytical techniques:

  • Image Classification: Supervised, unsupervised, or object-based
  • Change Detection: Image differencing, post-classification comparison, time series analysis
  • Spectral Indices: NDVI, NDWI, NDBI calculation and interpretation
  • Spatial Analysis: Zonal statistics, distance calculations, neighborhood analysis
  • Statistical Analysis: Regression, correlation, trend analysis

Step 4: Validation Strategy

Plan how you'll assess accuracy:

  • Ground Truth Data: Field observations, GPS points, existing maps
  • Reference Data: High-resolution imagery, historical records
  • Accuracy Metrics: Overall accuracy, kappa coefficient, producer's/user's accuracy
  • Uncertainty Quantification: Error propagation, confidence intervals

Stating Expected Hypotheses

In addition to research questions and methodology, it's important to state the expected hypotheses for your study. Hypotheses are statements that predict the outcome of your research based on existing knowledge and observations.

What Makes a Good Hypothesis?

  • Testable: Can be supported or refuted with data
  • Specific: Makes clear predictions
  • Grounded: Based on theory or prior observations
  • Falsifiable: Can be proven wrong

Example Hypotheses

Research Question: "How has land use and land cover changed in the Amazon Rainforest over the past decade?"

Hypothesis: "Land use in the Amazon Rainforest has increased over the past decade, leading to a decrease in forest cover (>20% loss) and an increase in agricultural land (>15% gain), primarily along road networks and in the Brazilian states of Pará and Mato Grosso."

This hypothesis is based on existing knowledge of deforestation in the Amazon Rainforest and can be tested using remote sensing data.

Research Question: "What is the relationship between urban heat island intensity and vegetation cover?"

Hypothesis: "Urban areas with higher vegetation cover (NDVI > 0.5) will exhibit land surface temperatures 3-5°C lower than areas with low vegetation cover (NDVI < 0.2), with the strongest cooling effect observed in parks larger than 5 hectares."

Incorporating Context and Broader Impacts

It is important to consider the broader impacts of research when writing research questions. This means thinking about how the research could be used to improve people's lives or protect the environment.

Types of Broader Impacts

Societal Benefits

  • Inform policy decisions on land use planning
  • Support disaster preparedness and response
  • Guide conservation priorities
  • Improve agricultural practices and food security
  • Enhance public health surveillance

Scientific Advancement

  • Develop new methods or algorithms
  • Validate existing theories
  • Create reusable datasets
  • Advance Earth Engine capabilities

Educational Value

  • Training materials for students
  • Public awareness campaigns
  • Citizen science opportunities
  • Visualization tools for education

Potential Negative Impacts

Be aware of potential negative consequences:

  • Misinterpretation by non-experts
  • Privacy concerns with high-resolution imagery
  • Unintended policy consequences
  • Resource extraction in sensitive areas

By considering context, broader impacts, and hypotheses, researchers can write research questions that are well-suited to remote sensing and can lead to valuable insights.

Typical Research Project Timeline

Weeks 1-2: Planning Phase

  • Literature review
  • Define research questions and hypotheses
  • Identify data sources

Weeks 3-4: Data Acquisition and Preprocessing

  • Access and download/load datasets
  • Apply preprocessing (cloud masking, correction)
  • Create initial visualizations

Weeks 5-7: Analysis

  • Implement analysis methods
  • Iterative refinement of approach
  • Generate results and statistics

Weeks 8-9: Validation and Interpretation

  • Accuracy assessment
  • Interpret results in context of research questions
  • Identify limitations

Weeks 10-12: Communication

  • Create figures and visualizations
  • Write report/paper
  • Prepare presentation
  • Share code and data
