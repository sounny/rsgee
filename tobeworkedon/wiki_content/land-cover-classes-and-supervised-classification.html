What Is Image Classification?

At its core, image classification is about sorting pixels in an image into different categories or classes. Think of a satellite photo of a city. There are roads, buildings, parks, and water bodies. The main goal of image classification is to automatically tell the computer how to distinguish between these different features. For example, let's say you're looking at a Landsat 8 image of the Earth. It's a beautiful tapestry of colors and shapes, but what does it all mean? Image classification helps you sort those pixels into categories like "forest," "water," and "urban area."

Why Choose a Classification Method?

Here's the big question: Which method should you use for classification? A lot of people stick with what they know. Maybe they learned a particular method in a class or found it easier to implement. While comfort is great, it doesn't necessarily mean that the method you're familiar with suits your specific project. You have various ways to approach classification, each with pros and cons. The key is to compare different methods to determine which works best for your project. Good research often involves testing multiple methods to see how they stack against each other.

Data Access and Preparation

Before you jump into classification, you need to find the right data. Whether using Google Earth Engine, ENVI, ERDAS, ArcGIS, or another platform, you'll spend significant time searching for, downloading, and preparing your data. For instance, with Google Earth Engine, you can access a lot of pre-processed data, making life easier. In contrast, if you're using another platform, you may need to search for images manually, download them, and prep them for analysis.

Types of Classification Methods

Supervised Classification

In supervised classification, you play an active role in guiding the algorithm. You provide a training sample‚Äîa set of points where you know the land cover. The algorithm then uses this sample to classify the rest of the image. Imagine you're teaching a toddler shapes by showing them examples of circles, squares, and triangles. Once they've seen enough examples, they can identify these shapes independently.  When you create a training sample, you make the wood template for the AI Toddler in the computer to put shapes through.

Unsupervised Classification

Unsupervised classification is like letting a toddler sort shapes without any prior instruction. You give the computer your image and tell it to sort the pixels into a certain number of classes. The computer then analyzes the entire image and groups similar pixels into these classes. Later, you label these classes manually, like saying, "Ah, this group of pixels represents water, and this group represents forest."

Object-based Image Analysis (OBIA)

Object-based image analysis is a more advanced form of classification. Instead of looking at individual pixels, the algorithm looks at groups of pixels, or "objects," and classifies those. This approach is particularly useful when you have high-resolution imagery where the context around a pixel helps determine what it represents.

Land Use vs. Land Cover

Remember, land use is not the same as land cover. Land cover is what you see‚Äîlike forest or water. Land use is how that land cover is being used. For instance, a forest might be used for conservation or logging. Be cautious when assigning human activities to land cover categories, as it involves interpretation and could be subject to error.

Evolution of Classification Methods

Over time, classification methods have evolved. Initially, we had limited data and simpler algorithms. With better sensors and higher-resolution imagery, we've developed more advanced methods like object-based image analysis. It's an exciting time to be in this field! There are a lot of research opportunities to compare methods and see which ones work for which scenarios. 

Practical Tips and Summary

  1. Quality of Data: Whether it's supervised or unsupervised classification, the quality of your data is crucial. You can always aim for accurate and representative training samples.

  2. Types of Data: You'll encounter discrete data (like the number of students in a class) and continuous data (like grades). Remote sensing generally starts with continuous data, which classification turns into discrete categories.

  3. Future Outlook: With advancements in AI and machine learning, we can expect even more sophisticated classification methods in the future.
